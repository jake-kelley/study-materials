CompTIA Security+ (SY0-701)

The industry-standard baseline cybersecurity certification — vendor-neutral, DoD 8140/8570 approved, and the most common "you know security fundamentals" credential for analysts, admins, and anyone entering infosec. It validates core skills across threats, cryptography, architecture, operations, and governance/risk.

≤90 questions 90 minutes pass 750/900 MC + PBQs Valid 3 years

Part 1 — High-Level Overview

What & Why

Security+ proves you can assess an organization's security posture, monitor and secure environments, understand threats and cryptography, and operate within governance/risk/compliance frameworks. It's often the first "real" security cert people earn and satisfies DoD baseline requirements — which is why it's so widely required in job postings.

Prerequisites None required. CompTIA recommends Network+ and ~2 years of security/systems-admin experience, but anyone can sit it. Detail in §1.

Part 2 — Thorough Study Guide

1 · Exam Details & PBQs

  • Up to 90 questions in 90 minutes. Passing score is 750 / 900 — a scaled score, so it's not simply "get 83% right."
  • Performance-based questions (PBQs): ~3–6 interactive simulations (drag-and-drop, configure a firewall/ACL, analyze a log or packet capture, match attacks to mitigations). They're presented first, are time-consuming, offer partial credit, and can be flagged/skipped.
  • Strategy: glance at each PBQ, flag the heavy ones, clear all the multiple-choice quickly, then return to the PBQs with your remaining time. Never leave a PBQ blank — partial credit.
  • Recommended experience: Network+ and ~2 years of security/admin work (not required). Valid 3 years; renew via 50 CEUs + maintenance fee, or by earning a higher CompTIA cert.
The #1 exam trap Questions ask for the BEST / MOST likely / FIRST answer — several options are often technically valid. Read the whole stem and every option; watch keywords FIRST, BEST, MOST, LEAST, NOT. It tests judgment in scenarios, not rote recall.

2 · General Security Concepts 12%

Security controls — two axes

Category (how it's implemented)Type (what it does)
Technical (tech/logical), Managerial (policy/risk), Operational (people/process), Physical (real-world)Preventive, Deterrent, Detective, Corrective, Compensating, Directive

Example: a firewall is a technical preventive control; a warning sign is physical deterrent; a backup is technical corrective.

Fundamental concepts

  • CIA triad — Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability. Plus Non-repudiation (can't deny an action — provided by digital signatures).
  • AAA — Authentication (who you are), Authorization (what you may do), Accounting (logging what you did). Applies to people and systems.
  • Zero Trust — "never trust, always verify." Split into a Control Plane (adaptive identity, policy engine, policy administrator, threat-scope reduction) and a Data Plane (subject/system, policy enforcement point, implicit trust zones).
  • Gap analysis; defense in depth; least privilege.
  • Physical security — bollards, access-control vestibule (mantrap), fencing, lighting, cameras, guards, badges, sensors (infrared/pressure/microwave/ultrasonic).
  • Deception tech — honeypot, honeynet, honeyfile, honeytoken.

Change management

Approval process, ownership, stakeholders, impact analysis, test results, backout plan, maintenance windows, SOPs; technical implications (allow/deny lists, restarts, downtime, dependencies, legacy apps); documentation & version control. (Cryptography basics are also introduced here — see the full treatment in §7.1.)

3 · Threats, Vulnerabilities & Mitigations 22%

Threat actors & motivations

  • Actors: nation-state (APT), unskilled attacker (script kiddie), hacktivist, insider threat, organized crime, shadow IT.
  • Attributes: internal/external, resources/funding, sophistication.
  • Motivations: data exfiltration, espionage, service disruption, blackmail, financial gain, philosophical/political, revenge, war, chaos.

Threat vectors & social engineering

  • Vectors/attack surfaces: message-based (email/SMS/IM), image/file/voice, removable media, vulnerable/unsupported software, unsecure networks (wireless/wired/Bluetooth), open ports, default credentials, supply chain (MSPs/vendors).
  • Human/social engineering: phishing, vishing, smishing, pretexting, impersonation, business email compromise (BEC), watering hole, brand impersonation, typosquatting, mis/disinformation.

Vulnerabilities

Application (memory injection, buffer overflow, race condition/TOCTOU, malicious update); OS; web (SQLi, XSS); hardware (firmware, end-of-life, legacy); virtualization (VM escape, resource reuse); cloud; supply chain; cryptographic; misconfiguration; mobile (side-loading, jailbreaking); zero-day.

Indicators of malicious activity

Malware: ransomware, trojan, worm, spyware, virus, keylogger, logic bomb, rootkit, bloatware. Attacks: DDoS (amplified/reflected), DNS/ARP poisoning, on-path (MITM), credential replay, injection, privilege escalation, directory traversal, password spraying/brute force, downgrade, collision/birthday. Indicators: account lockout, impossible travel, concurrent sessions, resource consumption, out-of-cycle or missing logs. (Full attack reference in §7.2.)

Mitigation & hardening

Segmentation, access control (ACL/permissions), application allow-listing, isolation, patching, encryption, monitoring, least privilege, configuration enforcement, decommissioning. Hardening: endpoint protection, host-based firewall/HIPS, disable unused ports/protocols, change default passwords, remove unnecessary software.

4 · Security Architecture 18%

Architecture models

  • Cloud (shared responsibility matrix, hybrid, third-party), IaC, serverless, microservices, on-prem, centralized vs decentralized, containerization, virtualization, IoT, ICS/SCADA, RTOS, embedded, high availability.
  • Design considerations: availability, resilience, cost, scalability, ease of deployment/recovery, risk transference, patch availability, power/compute.
  • Network infra: physical isolation/air-gap, logical segmentation, SDN.

Securing the enterprise infrastructure

  • Device placement, security zones, attack surface; fail-open vs fail-closed; active vs passive; inline vs tap/monitor.
  • Appliances: jump server, proxy (forward/reverse), IDS/IPS, load balancer, sensors.
  • Port security: 802.1X, EAP. Firewalls: WAF, UTM, NGFW, Layer 4 vs Layer 7.
  • Secure access: VPN (IPSec/TLS), tunneling, SD-WAN, SASE.

Protecting data

  • Data states: at rest, in transit, in use. Classifications: public, private, sensitive, confidential, restricted, critical. Types: regulated, trade secret, IP, financial, PII/PHI.
  • Methods: encryption, hashing, masking, tokenization, obfuscation, segmentation, permission restrictions, geographic restrictions; sovereignty/geolocation.

Resilience & recovery

  • High availability (load balancing vs clustering); sites (hot/warm/cold), geographic dispersion, platform diversity, multi-cloud.
  • Backups (onsite/offsite, frequency, encryption, snapshots, replication, journaling); power (UPS, generators); testing (tabletop, failover, simulation, parallel processing).

5 · Security Operations 28% — biggest

Hardening & secure baselines

Establish/deploy/maintain secure baselines; harden targets (mobile, workstations, switches/routers, servers, cloud, ICS/SCADA, embedded, RTOS, IoT). Wireless: site surveys, heat maps, WPA3, 802.1X + RADIUS/EAP. Mobile: MDM, BYOD/COPE/CYOD. App security: input validation, secure cookies, static code analysis, code signing, sandboxing.

Asset & vulnerability management

  • Asset lifecycle: acquisition → assignment/classification → tracking/inventory → disposal (sanitization, destruction, certification, retention).
  • Vulnerability management: identification (vuln scan, static/dynamic analysis, threat feeds/OSINT, pen testing, responsible disclosure/bug bounty); analysis (false positive/negative, CVE, CVSS, prioritization, exposure factor); response (patching, segmentation, compensating controls, exceptions); validation (rescan, audit).

Monitoring & tooling

  • Activities: log aggregation, alerting, scanning, reporting, archiving, quarantine, alert tuning.
  • Tools: SIEM (aggregate/correlate logs), SOAR (automation/playbooks), antivirus, DLP, SNMP traps, NetFlow, vulnerability scanners, SCAP/benchmarks, EDR/XDR, DNS filtering, web filtering, email security (DMARC/DKIM/SPF), file integrity monitoring, NAC, UBA.

Identity & Access Management

Provisioning/de-provisioning, identity proofing, federation, SSO (LDAP, OAuth, SAML), attestation; access-control models (MAC, DAC, RBAC, rule-based, ABAC); MFA factors; password policy & managers, passwordless; PAM (just-in-time, vaulting, ephemeral credentials). Full detail in §7.3.

Automation & orchestration

Use cases: user/resource provisioning, guardrails, ticket creation, escalation, CI/testing, APIs. Benefits: efficiency, baseline enforcement, faster reaction, workforce multiplier. Considerations: complexity, cost, single point of failure, technical debt.

Incident response & forensics

  • IR process (7 steps): Preparation → Detection → Analysis → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned.
  • Testing: tabletop, simulation. Root cause analysis, threat hunting.
  • Digital forensics: legal hold, chain of custody, acquisition, preservation, order of volatility, e-discovery, reporting.
  • Investigation data sources: firewall/application/endpoint/OS/IDS-IPS/network logs, metadata, vuln scans, dashboards, packet captures.

6 · Security Program Management & Oversight 20%

Governance

  • Policies: AUP, information security, BCP, DRP, incident response, SDLC, change management.
  • Standards (password, access control, encryption, physical) & procedures (onboarding/offboarding, playbooks).
  • Governance structures (boards, committees), external considerations (regulatory/legal/industry), data roles (owner, controller, processor, custodian/steward).

Risk management

  • Identification; assessment (ad hoc/recurring/continuous); analysis (qualitative vs quantitative).
  • Quantitative formulas: SLE = AV × EF; ALE = SLE × ARO. BIA metrics: RTO, RPO, MTTR, MTBF.
  • Risk register (KRIs, risk owners, thresholds); risk tolerance & appetite (expansionary/neutral/conservative); strategies: Accept, Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate.

Third-party risk

Vendor assessment (pen testing, right-to-audit, independent assessments, supply-chain analysis); agreements — SLA, MOA, MOU, MSA, SOW/WO, NDA, BPA; ongoing monitoring & questionnaires; rules of engagement.

Compliance & audits

  • Compliance reporting (internal/external); consequences (fines, sanctions, reputational/contractual/license loss); privacy (data subject, controller vs processor, right to be forgotten, retention).
  • Audits/assessments: attestation, internal (audit committee, self-assessment), external (regulatory, third-party), and pen testing (known/partially-known/unknown environment; passive vs active reconnaissance; offensive/defensive/physical).

Security awareness

Phishing campaigns & recognition, anomalous behavior recognition, user training (password mgmt, removable media, social engineering, operational security, remote/hybrid work), reporting & monitoring.

7 · High-Yield Reference

The topics below span multiple domains and appear constantly. Memorize them cold — much of the exam (and the PBQs) draws from here.

7.1 · Cryptography

TypePurposeExamples
SymmetricOne shared key; fast; bulk data / confidentialityAES (128/192/256), ChaCha20, 3DES; avoid DES/RC4
AsymmetricPublic/private key pair; key exchange & digital signatures; slowerRSA, ECC/ECDSA (efficient, mobile/IoT), Diffie-Hellman (key exchange)
HashingIntegrity; one-way; fixed lengthSHA-2/256/512, SHA-3; avoid MD5 (collisions); HMAC = hash + key
  • Digital signature = hash of the message encrypted with the sender's private key → integrity + authentication + non-repudiation. Encrypt for confidentiality with the recipient's public key.
  • Password protection: salt (defeats rainbow tables) + key stretching (bcrypt, PBKDF2, Argon2).
  • PKI: CA, RA, CSR, X.509 certs, CRL (revocation list) vs OCSP (real-time status), root of trust, wildcard/SAN certs, key escrow. TLS/HTTPS is hybrid — asymmetric to exchange a symmetric session key.
  • Other: TPM (on-device key storage), HSM (dedicated key hardware), secure enclave; obfuscation via steganography, tokenization, data masking; blockchain/open public ledger.

7.2 · Attacks

  • Social: phishing / spear phishing / whaling / vishing / smishing / pharming, watering hole, typosquatting, BEC.
  • Network: DDoS (amplified/reflected), on-path (MITM), DNS poisoning, ARP poisoning, replay, pass-the-hash.
  • Application: SQL injection, XSS (stored/reflected/DOM), CSRF, buffer overflow, race condition (TOCTOU), directory traversal, privilege escalation.
  • Password: spraying vs brute force vs dictionary; cryptographic: birthday, collision, downgrade.

7.3 · Identity & Access Management

  • MFA factors: something you know (password), have (token/smart card), are (biometric), somewhere you are (geolocation), something you do (behavior). MFA = two or more different factors.
  • SSO / federation protocols: SAML (enterprise web SSO), OAuth 2.0 (authorization), OpenID Connect (authentication on OAuth), Kerberos (tickets/TGT, port 88), LDAP/LDAPS. RADIUS vs TACACS+ (TACACS+ separates AAA, uses TCP, Cisco).
  • Access-control models: DAC (owner decides), MAC (labels/clearance), RBAC (role), ABAC (attributes), rule-based, time-of-day.
  • Principles: least privilege, separation of duties; PAM — just-in-time access, password vaulting, ephemeral credentials.

7.5 · Risk, BC & Frameworks

  • Quantitative risk: SLE = AV × EF; ALE = SLE × ARO. Risk strategies: Accept / Avoid / Transfer (insurance) / Mitigate.
  • Business continuity metrics: RTO (max time to restore), RPO (max data loss), MTBF (mean time between failures — repairable), MTTR (mean time to repair), MTTF (non-repairable lifespan).
  • Frameworks: NIST CSF = Identify, Protect, Detect, Respond, Recover (2.0 adds Govern); NIST RMF; NIST 800-53 (federal), 800-171 (CUI); CIS Controls (18)/Benchmarks; ISO 27001/27002; SOC 2; PCI DSS (cardholder data); MITRE ATT&CK, Cyber Kill Chain, Diamond Model; OWASP. Regs: GDPR, HIPAA, SOX.
Acronym soup CompTIA publishes a huge acronym appendix and expects you to know it. Build an Anki deck of acronyms + one-line definitions — it's the single highest-ROI recall tool for this exam.

9 · Study Plan (Retention-Based, ≈3–5 weeks)

Security+ is broad and acronym-heavy, which makes it the ideal candidate for spaced repetition (Anki) and retrieval practice. Cramming a vocabulary this large fails; distributed, self-tested review succeeds — and it's how you'll actually retain the concepts on the job.

10 · Tips & Common Traps

  • PBQs first, but do them last. Flag the heavy ones, clear the multiple-choice, then return. Never leave a PBQ blank (partial credit).
  • Pick the BEST / MOST likely / FIRST answer — several options are often technically correct. Watch keywords FIRST, BEST, MOST, LEAST, NOT.
  • Acronym soup — memorize the acronym list cold (that's what Anki is for).
  • Symmetric vs asymmetric vs hashing — and what a digital signature provides (integrity + auth + non-repudiation).
  • CRL vs OCSP, RADIUS vs TACACS+, DAC/MAC/RBAC/ABAC, IDS vs IPS, SIEM vs SOAR, EDR vs XDR.
  • Risk math: SLE = AV×EF, ALE = SLE×ARO; know RTO/RPO/MTBF/MTTR.
  • IR order: Preparation → Detection → Analysis → Containment → Eradication → Recovery → Lessons Learned.
  • The real exam often feels less nitpicky than Dion's practice tests — don't panic if Dion scores feel low.
Avoid dumps — seriously "SY0-701 real questions PDF" dump sites violate CompTIA's Candidate Agreement — penalties include a 12-month ban, score invalidation, and decertification (sometimes caught years later via answer-pattern audits). They're also often outdated (601 vs 701) and teach nothing for PBQs or the job. Legitimate practice exams (Dion, Messer, CertMaster) are original questions and are fine — dumps are stolen live questions.
